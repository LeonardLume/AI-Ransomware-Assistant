## When to Use
Use this skill when the user asks what should happen during a suspected ransomware incident or when assessing whether the organization has a ransomware-specific response plan.

## What to Ask
- Who is authorized to declare a serious cyber incident?
- Who must be contacted first: IT, MSP, leadership, legal, insurer, or CERT?
- Are emergency contact details available outside email and chat systems?
- Are there pre-agreed steps for isolating affected systems safely?
- Who decides when systems can be restored?
- Are communications to employees, customers, and partners prepared?

## Risk Logic
Low risk means the organization has a short ransomware response playbook, named decision makers, reachable contacts, and practiced containment and recovery coordination. Medium risk means some contacts or steps exist but are not complete. High risk means response depends on ad hoc decisions during the crisis. Critical risk means there is no known plan, no contact path, or no clear authority to act.

## Recommended Actions
- Write a one-page ransomware response checklist.
- Store emergency contacts outside the normal IT environment.
- Agree who can make containment, communication, legal, and recovery decisions.
- Define how to preserve key business records and incident notes.
- Coordinate with the MSP or IT provider before an incident occurs.
- Review when to contact CERT-EE, law enforcement, legal counsel, and insurance.

## Evidence Checklist
- Ransomware response checklist.
- Emergency contact list with offline copy.
- Named incident lead and backup decision maker.
- MSP or IT provider escalation procedure.
- Legal, insurance, and CERT contact notes.
- Internal communication template.
- Incident log template.

## Client Explanation
In a ransomware event, speed and clarity matter. The goal is not to improvise technical details under stress, but to know who decides, who calls whom, and how recovery work starts safely.
