## When to Use
Use this skill when assessing whether the organization has a documented and usable plan for serious cyber incidents, especially ransomware.

## What to Ask
- Is there a written incident response plan?
- Does the plan include ransomware scenarios?
- Who owns the plan and keeps it current?
- Are roles, contacts, and escalation paths documented?
- Is the plan available if email, file shares, or collaboration tools are unavailable?
- Has the plan been reviewed or tested in the last 12 months?

## Risk Logic
Low risk means the plan is documented, ransomware-specific, owned, accessible, and tested. Medium risk means a plan exists but is generic or outdated. High risk means only informal knowledge exists. Critical risk means staff do not know who leads a serious incident or how to reach key contacts.

## Recommended Actions
- Create or update a short incident response plan.
- Add ransomware-specific decision points, contacts, and recovery coordination.
- Store an offline or external copy of the plan.
- Assign a plan owner and review schedule.
- Add communication, legal, insurance, and regulator contact paths.
- Test the plan with a simple tabletop exercise.

## Evidence Checklist
- Incident response plan document.
- Ransomware-specific checklist or appendix.
- Named plan owner.
- Offline or external copy location.
- Contact and escalation list.
- Last review date.
- Last test or exercise record.

## Client Explanation
An incident response plan is the organization’s crisis checklist. It does not need to be long, but it must tell people who leads, who is contacted, and what the first safe steps are.
